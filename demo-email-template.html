CEO CORNER

The government shutdown continues; fortunately, so does the tremendous support our furloughed and working-without-pay federal employees are getting from a multitude of sources, including our member banks. We remain proud of all our banks that have stepped up and provided a safety net for these resilient Americans. Notably, USAA's $15 million donation to Coast Guard Mutual Assistance has set an extraordinary example of how, when the going gets tough, Americans help Americans.

As financially difficult and, in some cases, devastating this government shutdown continues to be, our focus this week is on tax season. As we've discussed several times in this newsletter, 2018 is the first year of the new tax law signed in 2017 - The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act. It's important that everyone understands how this new law has changed a tax code that has been relatively static over the past several years. Some fo those changes that will likely affect military families are summarized here.

Lower Tax Rates: This is the most fundamental and potentially beneficial change to the tax code. While the new law maintains the traditional seven tax brackets, those brackets, as well as the income ranges with each, have changed. It's likely that most military families will see a reduction in their tax rates. Whether that translates into an actual reduction in taxes depend, of course , on the rest of the family's financial picture.
